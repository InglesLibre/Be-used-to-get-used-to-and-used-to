Peruvian Lifestyles

used to · be used to · get used to in context

Peru is a country of contrasts, and the way people live varies greatly depending on where they are from and what they do. When I first moved from a small village in the Andes to study at a university in Lima, everything felt overwhelming. I wasn't used to the constant noise of traffic, and I couldn't get used to the fast pace of life. However, after a few months, I got used to taking crowded buses and studying late into the night. Now, as a university student, I am used to balancing my classes with part-time work.

My cousin Maria is a professional architect in Arequipa. She used to work long hours in a traditional office, but her company recently switched to remote work. At first, she struggled to get used to managing her own schedule, but now she is used to starting her day early and finishing in time to pick up her children from school.

In contrast, my grandparents still live in the Sacred Valley. They are used to waking up at dawn to tend to their crops and animals. They used to travel to Cusco only once a month, but now they go more often because the roads are better. Life there is slower, and they are not used to the chaos of big cities. Whenever they visit us in Lima, they always say they can't get used to the noise and the crowds.

Each lifestyle in Peru has its own rhythm. Whether you are used to the calm of the countryside or the energy of the city, what matters is finding the place where you belong. Over time, we all learn to adapt.

✅ be used to + noun/-ing
